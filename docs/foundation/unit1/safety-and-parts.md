# Module 1.1: Safety & Common Parts

Before you touch a single piece of metal, you need to know two things: how to stay safe, and what you're working with. This module covers workshop safety, every common VEX V5 part, and the tools you'll use to assemble them.

---

## Safety First

!!! danger "Non-Negotiable Safety Rules"
    These rules are not suggestions. Violating them will get you removed from the workshop.

    1. **Common sense is your best tool.** If something feels dangerous, stop and ask.
    2. **Clean up after yourself.** Every session ends with a clean workspace. No exceptions.
    3. **Use tools properly.** Screwdrivers are not pry bars. Wrenches are not hammers.
    4. **Safety glasses are required** in designated zones (grinding, cutting, drilling).
    5. **No running** in the workshop area.
    6. **No loose clothing or dangling jewelry** near rotating machinery.
    7. **Report injuries immediately** — even small cuts or pinches.

### Shop Tour

Before any building begins, your mentor will walk you through the workspace. Pay attention to:

- **Tool stations** — where each tool lives and where it goes back
- **Parts bins** — organized by type, keep them organized
- **Safety equipment locations** — first aid kit, safety glasses, fire extinguisher
- **Restricted zones** — areas requiring safety glasses or mentor supervision
- **Charging station** — V5 Battery charging protocols

## V5 Common Tools and Uses

| Tool | Used For | Tips |
|------|----------|------|
| **Star Drive Screwdriver** (T15) | Driving all VEX star-drive screws | Don't strip the head — press firmly while turning |
| **Combination Wrench** (11/32") | Tightening keps nuts and nylock nuts | Hold the nut, turn the screw (or vice versa) |
| **Allen Keys** (various) | Set screws, shaft collars | Keep the full set together — you'll need multiple sizes |
| **Needle-Nose Pliers** | Holding small nuts in tight spaces | Not a substitute for a wrench |
| **Wire Cutters/Strippers** | Cutting zip ties, trimming wires | Cut away from yourself |
| **Ruler / Calipers** | Measuring spacing, alignment | Holes are 0.5" apart — use the hole pattern as your ruler |

!!! tip "The Holes = Inches Trick"
    On VEX metal, **every 2 holes = 1 inch**. A 25-hole C-Channel is 12.5 inches long. Count holes instead of reaching for a ruler — it's faster and more accurate for VEX builds.

## Structural Metal: Channels and Beams

VEX structural metal comes in several profiles. Each has a specific use case.

### C-Channels

C-Channels are the **backbone of every robot**. They are C-shaped in cross-section and come in various widths.

| Type | Cross Section Width | Common Uses |
|------|-------------------|-------------|
| **2x C-Channel** | 2 holes wide | Drivetrain rails, mechanism arms |
| **3x C-Channel** | 3 holes wide | Wider structural members, motor mounts |
| **5x C-Channel** | 5 holes wide | Chassis base plates, large structural elements |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    2-wide, 3-wide, and 5-wide C-Channels side by side showing cross-section profiles

### L-Channels

L-shaped metal used for bracing and mounting.

| Type | Description | Common Uses |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **1x L-Channel** | Single-row angle bracket | Light bracing, small brackets |
| **2x L-Channel** | Two-row angle bracket | Structural bracing, motor mounting |

### U-Channels

| Type | Description | Common Uses |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **2x U-Channel** | U-shaped, 2 holes wide | Enclosing axles, protective housings |

### Flat Beams

Flat strips of metal with mounting holes.

| Type | Description | Common Uses |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **1x Flat Beam** | Single-row flat strip | Light cross-bracing, linkages |
| **5x Flat Beam** | Five-row flat plate | Base plates, large mounting surfaces |

## Screws

VEX uses **star drive** screws (T15 head). There are two main types, and understanding the difference is critical.

### Shoulder Screws vs. Standard Screws

| Feature | Shoulder Screw | Standard Screw |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Shaft** | Smooth unthreaded shoulder below the head | Fully threaded from head to tip |
| **Use case** | When parts need to **rotate** on the screw (pivot points, screw joints) | When parts need to be **locked together** rigidly |
| **Friction** | Low — smooth shoulder allows rotation | High — threads grip the metal |
| **Identification** | Smooth section visible below head | Threads run all the way up |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    Shoulder screw vs standard screw side by side — arrow showing smooth shoulder vs full threading

!!! warning "Don't Mix Them Up"
    Using a standard screw where you need rotation will create binding and friction. Using a shoulder screw where you need rigidity will create a wobbly joint. **Know which one you're grabbing.**

### Screw Lengths

Screws come in various lengths. The most common:

- **1/4"** — single layer attachments
- **3/8"** — two layers of metal
- **1/2"** — two layers with spacer or standoff
- **3/4" and 1"** — thick stacks, motor mounts, multi-layer assemblies
- **1.5" and 2"** — boxing long assemblies, standoff stacks

## Nuts

| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Keps Nut** | Star-washer built into the base — grips and resists loosening | General-purpose attachment, most connections |
| **Nylock Lock Nut** | Nylon insert creates friction lock — very secure | High-vibration areas, joints that must never loosen |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    Keps nut and nylock nut side by side showing star washer vs nylon insert

!!! tip "Wrench Technique"
    Hold the nut with your wrench and turn the screw with your star driver. For tight spaces, flip it — hold the screw head and turn the nut. Find what works for the situation.

!!! example "Activity: Screw Comparison Build"
    **Time:** 10 minutes | **Format:** Individual

    1. Take two short C-Channels and attach them at a single point using a **standard screw + keps nut**. Tighten fully. Try to wiggle the joint.
    2. Now do the same with a **shoulder screw + keps nut**. Tighten fully. Try to wiggle the joint.
    3. Notice the difference: the shoulder screw joint allows rotation even when tight. The standard screw joint is locked rigid.
    4. Try replacing the keps nut with a **nylock nut** on each. Feel the extra resistance.

## Spacers and Washers

Spacers and washers are small but critical. They control the **position, friction, and stability** of every rotating and structural component.

### Spacers

Spacers are plastic cylinders that slide onto screws or axles to create precise gaps between components.

| Size | Color (typically) | Common Use |
|------|------------------|------------|
| **1/16"** | Thin black | Fine adjustment |
| **1/8"** | Black | Standard spacing between components |
| **1/4"** | White/Black | Larger gaps, bearing positioning |
| **3/8"** | White | Wide spacing |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    All spacer sizes (1/16, 1/8, 1/4, 3/8 inch) lined up with labels

### Washers

Thin metal discs that distribute load and reduce friction between a screw head and the metal surface.

### Why They Matter

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Two rotating parts rubbing together | Add a spacer between them |
| A screw head digging into soft material | Add a washer under the head |
| An axle wobbling side-to-side | Fill the gap with spacers |
| A gear grinding against a C-Channel | Space the gear away from the rail |

!!! warning "The #1 Beginner Mistake"
    Beginners skip spacers to "save time." This causes friction, binding, misalignment, and parts wearing out. **Every gap needs to be filled intentionally** — either with a spacer or with a deliberate clearance.

## Standoffs

Standoffs are **threaded aluminum cylinders** that connect two parallel surfaces with precise spacing. They screw onto screws from both ends.

### Common Sizes

| Length | Common Use |
|--------|-----------|
| **1/2"** | Short connections, bracing |
| **1"** | Medium structural connections |
| **2"** | Tall structural supports |
| **Custom (7/8")** | Boxing C-Channels (see below) |

### Boxing with Standoffs

**Boxing** is the technique of connecting two parallel C-Channels face-to-face with standoffs between them, creating a rigid box beam structure.

The magic number for boxing two 2-wide C-Channels is **7/8" standoffs** — this is the exact interior spacing that creates a tight, rigid box.

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    Two C-Channels boxed together with standoffs, viewed from end showing box-beam cross-section

!!! example "📐 Diagram Needed"
    Cross-section of boxed C-Channel: two channels face-to-face with standoff between, screws labeled

!!! example "Activity: Build a Boxed C-Channel"
    **Time:** 15 minutes | **Format:** Individual

    1. Take two matching C-Channels (same length).
    2. Using 7/8" standoffs (or combine sizes to get close), connect the two C-Channels face-to-face at both ends and at least one middle point.
    3. Use screws through both C-Channel faces, threading into the standoff.
    4. Test rigidity: try to twist and flex the assembly. Compare to a single C-Channel.

    **Result:** The boxed assembly should be dramatically stiffer than a single C-Channel.

### Why Boxing Matters

| Single C-Channel | Boxed C-Channel |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Flexes under load | Resists bending and twisting |
| Can wobble side-to-side | Rigid in all directions |
| Lightweight | Slightly heavier but much stronger |
| Fine for light mechanisms | Required for drivetrain rails and loaded arms |

!!! tip "One Long Screw vs. Two Short Screws"
    When boxing, you can either use **one long screw** that passes through both C-Channels and the standoff, or **two short screws** that each thread into opposite ends of the standoff. Two short screws are generally preferred — they're easier to assemble and disassemble.

!!! example "Activity: Build Your Initials"

## Steel vs. Aluminum

VEX structural metal comes in both steel and aluminum.

| Property | Steel | Aluminum |
|----------|-------|----------|
| **Weight** | Heavy | ~1/3 the weight |
| **Strength** | Very strong | Strong enough for most applications |
| **Flexibility** | Stiff | Slightly more flex |
| **Cost** | Same | Same |
| **Best for** | High-load areas (drivetrain rails) | Everything else (superstructure, mechanisms) |

!!! tip "Competition Strategy"
    Most competitive robots use **steel for the drivetrain** (where rigidity matters most) and **aluminum for everything above** (to save weight). Weight affects acceleration, turning speed, and battery life.

## Bearings

Bearings press into the square holes of C-Channels and provide a smooth, centered bore for axles to spin through.

### Types

| Type | Bore Size | Used With |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Low Strength Bearing** | 1/8" square bore | Low-strength (1/8") axles |
| **High Strength Bearing** | 1/4" square bore | High-strength (1/4") axles |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    Bearing pressed into C-Channel square hole with axle passing through

### Why Bearings Matter

Without a bearing, an axle spinning inside a square hole has:

- **Poor centering** — the round axle rattles in the square hole
- **High friction** — metal-on-metal contact
- **Wear** — the hole gets enlarged over time

With a bearing:

- **Perfect centering** — the bearing bore matches the axle diameter
- **Low friction** — smooth plastic-on-metal contact
- **Durability** — the bearing takes the wear instead of the metal

!!! example "📐 Diagram Needed"
    Axle in bare hole (rattling) vs axle in bearing (centered, smooth) comparison

!!! warning "Always Use Bearings"
    There is never a reason to skip bearings on rotating axles. Press them into every hole where an axle passes through. It takes 5 seconds and saves hours of debugging friction problems.

## Motors and Cartridges

### V5 Smart Motors

VEX V5 has two motor sizes:

| Motor | Power | Default RPM | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------------|-------|
| **5.5W Motor** | 5.5 watts | 200 RPM | Smaller, lighter, fewer applications |
| **11W Motor** | 11 watts | 200 RPM | Standard competition motor, interchangeable cartridges |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    5.5W and 11W V5 Smart Motors side by side for size comparison

### Motor Cartridges (11W Only)

The 11W motor accepts **interchangeable gear cartridges** that change the output speed and torque:

| Cartridge Color | Output RPM | Torque | Best For |
|----------------|------------|--------|----------|
| **Red** (100 RPM) | 100 | Highest | Heavy lifts, clamps, high-load mechanisms |
| **Green** (200 RPM) | 200 | Medium | Drivetrains (default), general purpose |
| **Blue** (600 RPM) | 600 | Lowest | Flywheels, intakes, high-speed mechanisms |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    All 3 V5 motor cartridges (red 100RPM, green 200RPM, blue 600RPM) side by side

!!! tip "Cartridge + External Gear Ratio"
    The cartridge sets the motor's internal gear ratio. You can stack an **external gear ratio** on top of that for even more customization. For example: green cartridge (200 RPM) with a 2:1 external speed ratio = 400 RPM output.

## Shaft Collars

Shaft collars lock onto axles to prevent lateral (side-to-side) movement. They use a set screw to clamp onto the shaft.

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Clamping Collar** | Set screw tightens onto the shaft — most common |
| **Rubber Shaft Collar** | Friction fit — quick to reposition, less secure |

!!! tip "Collar Placement"
    Place shaft collars on both sides of components that shouldn't slide. The collar on each side acts as a wall, keeping the gear/wheel/sprocket exactly where you want it.

## Wheels

### Wheel Types

| Wheel | Key Feature | Best For |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **Traction Wheel** | Full rubber contact surface — maximum grip | Drivetrains that need pushing power, no-slip applications |
| **Omni Wheel** | Small perpendicular rollers around the circumference | Turning with less scrub, side-to-side movement |
| **Mecanum Wheel** | Angled rollers (45°) around the circumference | Omni-directional movement (strafe, diagonal, rotate) |

!!! example "📷 Photo Needed"
    Traction wheel, omni wheel, and mecanum wheel side by side with labels

### When to Use Each

| Drivetrain Type | Wheel Setup |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Standard tank drive** | 4 or 6 traction wheels, or mix traction + omni |
| **Easy-turn tank drive** | Omni wheels on front and back, traction in middle |
| **Holonomic / X-drive** | 4 omni wheels at 45° angles |
| **Mecanum drive** | 4 mecanum wheels (specific L/R pattern) |

!!! example "📐 Diagram Needed"
    Top-down diagrams: tank drive, easy-turn tank, X-drive, mecanum — showing wheel types and placement

!!! example "Activity: Friction Test Challenge"
    **Time:** 20 minutes | **Format:** Pairs

    **Objective:** Build a single free-spinning wheel on an axle and achieve **5+ seconds of continuous spin** from a single flick.

    **Requirements:**

    1. Mount a wheel on a high-strength axle
    2. Support the axle in bearings pressed into C-Channel
    3. Use spacers to eliminate lateral play
    4. Use shoulder screws or screw joints where appropriate

    **Procedure:**

    1. Build your assembly
    2. Flick the wheel and time how long it spins
    3. Iterate — adjust spacing, check bearing alignment, reduce friction
    4. Record your best time

    **Scoring:**

    | Time | Rating |
    |------|--------|
    | < 2 seconds | Needs work — friction is too high |
    | 2–4 seconds | Acceptable — room for improvement |
    | 5–7 seconds | Good — solid friction management |
    | 8+ seconds | Excellent — competition-quality assembly |

    **Hint:** If your wheel stops quickly, check for: axle rubbing on metal (use bearings), components pressed too tightly (add spacers), misaligned bearing holes.
