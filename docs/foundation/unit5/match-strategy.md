# Module 5.3: Match Strategy

**The best robot does not always win. The best team does.** Match strategy is about using information to make decisions — scouting opponents, selecting the right alliance partner, choosing the right role during a match, and communicating with your partner in real time. This module teaches you to think strategically, not just mechanically.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Scout other robots and record their capabilities
- Analyze scouting data to make alliance selection decisions
- Define and execute match roles (offensive vs defensive)
- Communicate effectively with alliance partners during matches
- Adapt strategy mid-tournament based on results

## Scouting

### What Is Scouting?

Scouting is the systematic observation and recording of other robots' performance during matches. It answers the question: "What can that robot do, and how well does it do it?"

### What to Scout

| Category | What to Observe | Why It Matters |
|----------|----------------|---------------|
| **Drivetrain** | Speed, turning ability, pushing power | Predicts mobility and matchup strength |
| **Mechanisms** | Which mechanisms, how fast, how reliable | Predicts scoring ability |
| **Autonomous** | Does it have one? Does it score? Does it collide? | Predicts autonomous period outcome |
| **Driver skill** | Smooth driving, quick reactions, good positioning | Driver skill wins close matches |
| **Reliability** | Does it break down? Does it stall? Disconnects? | Unreliable robots lose matches they should win |
| **Strategy** | Offensive or defensive? Aggressive or passive? | Predicts how they will play in your match |

### Scouting Sheet

Use a simple sheet for each robot you observe:

```
Robot: _______________    Match: _______________

Drivetrain:    [ ] Fast  [ ] Medium  [ ] Slow
Turning:       [ ] Agile  [ ] Average  [ ] Sluggish

  Has one:     [ ] Yes  [ ] No
  Scores:      [ ] Yes  [ ] No    Points: ___

Notes: ___________________________________
```

### Scouting Tips

!!! tip "Watch the Losses, Not Just the Wins"
    A robot that wins every match does not tell you much about its weaknesses. Watch their closest match or their loss — that is where you learn what they struggle with. Every robot has a weakness; your job is to find it.

- Scout every robot, not just the top teams
- Update your scouting data after every match (robots improve during tournaments)
- Watch for patterns: does a robot always struggle with a specific task?
- Note mechanical failures — a robot that broke once will likely break again

## Alliance Selection

### How Alliance Selection Works

In the Foundation Championship (and real VEX tournaments):

1. Teams are **seeded** based on their individual challenge performance
2. The **#1 seed picks first**, choosing any other team as their alliance partner
3. The **#2 seed picks next**, and so on
4. If a team is picked, they may **accept or decline** (in real VEX, they can decline once)
5. Alliances are locked for the elimination rounds

### How to Pick the Right Partner

| Factor | Weight | Why |
|--------|--------|-----|
| **Autonomous compatibility** | 30% | Two good autonomouses that do not collide = huge advantage |
| **Mechanism complementarity** | 25% | Partner should cover what you cannot do |
| **Driver skill** | 20% | A great driver with a mediocre robot beats a bad driver with a great robot |
| **Reliability** | 15% | A partner who disconnects mid-match costs you the round |
| **Communication** | 10% | A partner you can talk to and coordinate with in real time |

### Alliance Selection Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Is Wrong |
|---------|----------------|
| Picking the robot with the most mechanisms | More mechanisms = more things to break |
| Picking your friend regardless of performance | Friendship does not win matches — pick the best available partner |
| Ignoring autonomous compatibility | Two autonomouses that collide = both teams score 0 |
| Picking a fast robot without checking reliability | Speed without reliability = inconsistent results |
| Not scouting before selections | You are guessing instead of deciding |

## Match Roles

In 2v2 matches, each robot on an alliance should have a defined role. Trying to do the same thing leads to confusion, collisions, and missed opportunities.

### Offensive Role

The offensive robot focuses on **scoring points**:

- Collect and score game objects as fast as possible
- Target the highest-value scoring actions
- Avoid getting into pushing matches that waste time
- Use the entire match time for point-generating actions

**Best for:** Robots with fast, reliable scoring mechanisms

### Defensive Role

The defensive robot focuses on **preventing the opponents from scoring**:

- Block opponent robots from reaching game objects
- Push opponents away from scoring zones
- Use your wedge to control opponent positioning
- Protect scored objects from being taken back

**Best for:** Robots with strong drivetrains and good wedges, even if scoring mechanisms are slower

### Hybrid Role

Sometimes both robots play a hybrid role:

- Each covers half the field (left/right or front/back)
- Both score, but each focuses on different areas
- If one robot breaks down, the other can cover the whole field

**Best for:** Alliances where both robots have similar capabilities

### Role Assignment Checklist

Before each match, discuss with your partner:

1. "Who plays offense and who plays defense?"
2. "Which side of the field does each of us cover?"
3. "If the plan is not working at 30 seconds, what do we switch to?"
4. "What do we do if one of us breaks down?"

## Communication During Matches

### Pre-Match (30 seconds before start)

- Confirm roles: "I am offense, you are defense"
- Confirm autonomous: "My auto goes left, yours goes right"
- Confirm contingency: "If I break down, you switch to offense"

### During the Match

Effective communication during a 60-second match must be **short, loud, and clear**:

| Good Callout | What It Means |
|-------------|---------------|
| "I've got left side!" | I am covering the left, you cover the right |
| "Ball at the wall!" | There is an uncontested game object by the wall |
| "Block number 3!" | Prevent opponent robot #3 from scoring |
| "I'm stuck!" | I need you to come push me free |
| "Switch!" | Change to our backup plan |
| "Ten seconds!" | Time is almost up — last push |

### Communication Mistakes

| Mistake | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| Not talking at all | Robots duplicate effort or leave areas uncovered |
| Talking too much | Driver cannot focus on driving and talking |
| Using long sentences | Takes too long, driver misses the point |
| Giving vague instructions | "Get that thing!" — what thing? Where? |
| Disagreeing mid-match | Wastes time, creates confusion |

!!! tip "Practice Communication Before the Tournament"
    Run 2-3 practice matches with your alliance partner before the real tournament. The goal is not just to practice driving — it is to practice talking to each other under pressure. Develop a shared vocabulary so that one word communicates an entire strategy.

## Adapting Strategy Mid-Tournament

### Between Matches

After every match, do a 2-minute debrief with your partner:

1. **What worked?** — Keep doing it
2. **What failed?** — Fix it or change strategy
3. **What did the opponent do that we did not expect?** — Adapt
4. **Do we need to change roles?** — Maybe defense is not working
5. **Any mechanical issues to fix?** — Quick repairs between matches

### When to Change Strategy

| Signal | Adaptation |
|--------|-----------|
| Opponent's autonomous beats yours consistently | Change starting position or autonomous routine |
| Your intake keeps jamming | Switch to defense-focused play |
| Partner's robot is faster than expected | Let them play offense, you play defense |
| A specific opponent is dominating | Focus defense specifically on that robot |
| You are winning comfortably | Play conservative, do not risk breakdowns |
| You are losing and must win | Play aggressive, accept higher risk for higher reward |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Scouting is not optional** — information is the foundation of good strategy
2. **Pick partners based on data** — not friendship or reputation
3. **Define roles before the match** — both robots doing the same thing is worse than one doing nothing
4. **Communicate with short, clear callouts** — drivers cannot process paragraphs
5. **Adapt between matches** — the team that learns fastest wins the tournament
